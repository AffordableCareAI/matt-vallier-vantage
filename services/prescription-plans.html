Understanding Part D

What Is Medicare Part D?

Medicare Part D is the prescription drug benefit available to all Medicare beneficiaries. It is offered through private insurance companies approved by Medicare.

Original Medicare does not cover most prescriptions. If you have Medicare Part A and Part B without a Medicare Advantage plan that includes drug coverage, you are paying full retail price for your medications. A standalone Part D plan can save you hundreds or thousands of dollars per year.

Every Part D plan has a formulary. A formulary is the list of drugs your plan covers. Different plans cover different drugs at different prices. The plan that is cheapest for your neighbor may not be the cheapest for you — because you take different medications.

Plans change every year. Premiums, copays, deductibles, and formularies are updated annually. A plan that was perfect last year might not cover your drugs this year, or might charge more for them. That is why reviewing your Part D plan during Annual Enrollment (October 15 through December 7) is essential.

Not enrolling has consequences. If you do not sign up for Part D when you first become eligible and you do not have creditable drug coverage elsewhere, you may pay a late enrollment penalty that increases your monthly premium permanently.

The Inflation Reduction Act changed the game. Starting in 2025, Part D plans cap your total out-of-pocket drug spending at $2,000 per year. Once you hit that cap, your covered drugs cost $0 for the rest of the year. This is a major improvement for seniors with expensive medications.

How Part D Works

The Four Coverage Phases

Part D coverage is divided into four phases. Your out-of-pocket costs change as you move through each phase during the calendar year.

1

Deductible

You pay the full cost of your drugs until you meet the annual deductible (up to $590 in 2026). Some plans have $0 deductibles or waive deductibles for certain drug tiers.

2

Initial Coverage

After meeting your deductible, you pay copays or coinsurance for each prescription. The plan pays the rest. This phase continues until your combined drug costs reach $5,030.

3

Coverage Gap

Previously known as the “donut hole.” Thanks to the Inflation Reduction Act, out-of-pocket costs are significantly reduced in this phase as you approach the $2,000 annual cap.

4

Catastrophic Coverage

Once your out-of-pocket costs reach $2,000 for the year, you pay $0 for all covered drugs for the remainder of the calendar year. This cap was introduced by the Inflation Reduction Act.

Formulary Tiers

Understanding Drug Tiers

Part D plans organize covered drugs into tiers. Lower tiers have lower copays. Here is how the tier system typically works.

1

Preferred Generic

Lowest-cost generic drugs. Often $0 to $5 copay.

2

Generic

Other generic medications. Typically $5 to $15 copay.

3

Preferred Brand

Brand-name drugs on the preferred list. Around $30 to $50 copay.

4

Non-Preferred Brand

Brand-name drugs not on the preferred list. Higher copay, often 25–40% coinsurance.

5

Specialty

High-cost specialty drugs. Usually 25–33% coinsurance with the $2,000 cap protecting you.

Enrollment

Annual Enrollment: October 15 – December 7

Why You Must Review Your Part D Plan Every Year

Even if you are happy with your current plan, it may change for the next year. Here is what can shift:

  • Your medications may move to a higher (more expensive) tier
  • Your medications may be removed from the formulary entirely
  • Your monthly premium may increase
  • Your deductible or copays may change
  • A better plan may now be available in your area
  • Preferred pharmacies may change, affecting your copays

Part D FAQ

Medicare Part D is the prescription drug benefit available to all Medicare beneficiaries. It is offered through private insurance companies approved by Medicare. If you have Original Medicare (Parts A and B) without a Medicare Advantage plan that includes drug coverage, you need a standalone Part D plan to help cover the cost of your prescription medications.
The main enrollment window is the Annual Enrollment Period, which runs from October 15 through December 7 each year. You can also enroll when you first become eligible for Medicare (your Initial Enrollment Period), or if you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period due to certain life events such as moving, losing other coverage, or qualifying for Extra Help.
Part D has four phases: (1) Deductible — you pay full price until meeting the annual deductible. (2) Initial Coverage — you pay copays while the plan covers the rest. (3) Coverage Gap — reduced costs thanks to the Inflation Reduction Act. (4) Catastrophic Coverage — once your out-of-pocket costs reach $2,000 for the year, you pay $0 for covered drugs the rest of the year.
If you go 63 or more consecutive days without creditable prescription drug coverage, you may have to pay a late enrollment penalty. The penalty is calculated based on how many months you went without coverage and is added to your monthly Part D premium for as long as you have a Part D plan. It is important to enroll on time or ensure you have creditable coverage through another source.
