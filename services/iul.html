What Is Indexed Universal Life Insurance?

IUL is permanent life insurance with a cash value component that grows based on stock market index performance — without directly investing in the market.

Indexed Universal Life sits at the intersection of life insurance and wealth building. You pay a premium each month. Part of that premium covers the cost of insurance (your death benefit). The rest goes into a cash value account that earns interest based on the performance of a stock market index — most commonly the S&P 500.

The key difference from direct investing: your cash value has a floor (typically 0%) and a cap (typically 10–12%). If the S&P 500 drops 20% in a year, your credited interest is 0% — not negative. If the S&P 500 gains 25%, your credited interest is capped at 10–12%. You give up some upside to eliminate the downside.

Over time, this cash value accumulates tax-deferred. When you're ready to retire, you can access it through tax-free policy loans — creating a stream of income that doesn't show up on your tax return. That's the power of IUL: growth potential, downside protection, and tax advantages all wrapped in a permanent death benefit.

How IUL Works — Step by Step

Understanding the mechanics helps you decide if IUL belongs in your financial plan.

1

You Pay a Flexible Premium

Unlike whole life, IUL premiums are flexible. You can pay more in good years and less in lean ones, as long as you meet the minimum to keep the policy active. More premium means more cash value growth potential.

2

Cost of Insurance Is Deducted

Each month, the insurance company deducts the cost of insurance (COI) and administrative fees from your cash value. This pays for your death benefit. COI increases as you age, which is why proper funding in early years is critical.

3

Remaining Premium Grows in Cash Value

Everything above the COI goes into your cash value account. The insurance company credits interest based on the chosen index performance, subject to the floor (0%) and cap (10–12%). Your money is never directly in the market — the carrier uses options strategies to deliver index-linked returns.

4

Access Cash Value Tax-Free in Retirement

Once sufficient cash value has accumulated, you can take tax-free policy loans against it. These loans don't trigger income tax (unlike 401k/IRA withdrawals). The remaining cash value continues to earn interest, and the death benefit is reduced by outstanding loans.

Important Disclosures

IUL is a powerful tool when used correctly. But you need to understand the fine print before committing.

What You Need to Know Before Buying IUL

  • Cap rates can change. The insurance company sets the cap rate and can adjust it over time. A 12% cap today could be 9% next year. Your floor stays at 0%, but the upside ceiling is not permanently locked.
  • Fees reduce returns. Cost of insurance, administrative charges, and rider fees are deducted from your cash value monthly. In early years, these can significantly reduce your net growth. Proper funding minimizes this impact.
  • Nothing is guaranteed. Cash value projections are hypothetical. The illustration your agent shows you is not a promise — it's a projection based on assumptions that may or may not play out over 20+ years.
  • Underfunding is the biggest risk. If you don't pay enough premium to outpace the rising cost of insurance, your policy can lapse. IUL requires commitment and consistent funding to perform as illustrated.
  • Always get a carrier illustration. Before purchasing any IUL policy, request a formal illustration from the insurance carrier. Matt Vallier will walk you through every number, every assumption, and every fee so you understand exactly what you're buying.

Honest answers to the questions people actually ask about IUL.

Indexed Universal Life is a type of permanent life insurance that provides a death benefit for your beneficiaries plus a cash value component that grows based on the performance of a stock market index like the S&P 500. Unlike direct market investing, IUL policies have a floor (typically 0%) that protects your cash value from losses during market downturns, and a cap (typically 10–12%) that limits gains in strong years. This gives you market-linked growth potential without the risk of losing money when the market drops.
IUL offers several advantages over traditional retirement accounts. There are no annual contribution limits like the $23,500 cap on 401(k)s or $7,000 on IRAs. Cash value grows tax-deferred and can be accessed tax-free through policy loans, whereas 401(k) and IRA withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. IUL also includes a death benefit that retirement accounts don't provide. However, IUL has higher internal fees than most retirement accounts, and it requires proper illustration from the carrier to understand projected returns.
