Understanding Medicare

What Is Medicare & Why Does It Matter?

Medicare is the federal health insurance program for Americans age 65 and older, as well as certain younger individuals with disabilities or end-stage renal disease. It is your foundation for healthcare in retirement — but it does not cover everything.

Original Medicare has four parts, and understanding how they work together is the key to avoiding surprise medical bills:

  • Part A (Hospital Insurance) — Covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health services. Most people pay no premium for Part A because they or a spouse paid Medicare taxes for 10 or more years.
  • Part B (Medical Insurance) — Covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, durable medical equipment, and some home health services. The standard Part B premium is $185 per month in 2026, though higher earners pay more through IRMAA surcharges.
  • Part C (Medicare Advantage) — Private plans that combine Part A and Part B benefits into a single plan. Many also include Part D drug coverage plus extras like dental, vision, and hearing. Network-based.
  • Part D (Prescription Drug Coverage) — Standalone drug plans that work with Original Medicare and Medigap. Covers the cost of prescription medications with varying formularies and tier structures.

Here is the reality that catches many people off guard: Original Medicare (Parts A and B) still leaves you responsible for 20% of outpatient costs with no annual out-of-pocket maximum. A single hospital stay or cancer diagnosis could mean thousands of dollars in bills. That is exactly why supplemental coverage — either a Medigap plan or Medicare Advantage plan — is essential.

Compare Your Options

Medicare Supplement vs. Medicare Advantage

Two paths to filling the gaps in Original Medicare. The right choice depends on how you use healthcare, where you travel, and what matters most to you.

Medicare Supplement (Medigap)

Standardized federal plans that cover what Original Medicare does not. Same benefits regardless of which insurance company you choose — only the premium differs.

  • Standardized Plans A through N — Plan G is the most popular, covering nearly all out-of-pocket costs except the Part B deductible
  • See any doctor who accepts Medicare — no networks, no referrals, no prior authorizations
  • Predictable monthly costs — you know exactly what you will pay each month with minimal surprise bills
  • Works nationwide — ideal for snowbirds and travelers who split time between states
  • Guaranteed renewable — your plan cannot be canceled as long as you pay your premium

Requires a separate Part D plan for prescription drug coverage.

Medicare Advantage (MAPD)

All-in-one plans from private insurers that replace Original Medicare. Often include benefits that Original Medicare does not cover at all.

  • All-in-one coverage — combines Parts A, B, and usually D into a single plan with one card
  • Extra benefits included — many plans add dental, vision, hearing, fitness memberships, and over-the-counter allowances
  • Low or $0 monthly premiums — many Medicare Advantage plans have no additional premium beyond your Part B
  • Annual out-of-pocket maximum — your costs are capped each year, providing financial protection
  • Network-based care — HMO or PPO structure with copays instead of coinsurance for most services

Requires using in-network providers for lowest costs. Plan availability varies by county.

Common Questions

Medicare FAQ

Honest answers to the questions people actually ask about Medicare.

Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) starts 3 months before you turn 65, includes your birthday month, and ends 3 months after. This is your best window to enroll without penalties or underwriting. If you miss it, the General Enrollment Period runs January 1 through March 31 each year, with coverage beginning July 1. Medicare Advantage and Part D plans have an Annual Enrollment Period from October 15 to December 7 — this is when you can switch plans for the following year. Special Enrollment Periods are also available if you lose employer coverage, move to a new area, or experience other qualifying life events. Matt Vallier can help you identify exactly which enrollment window applies to your situation.
Medicare Supplement (Medigap) plans work alongside Original Medicare to cover your out-of-pocket costs — copays, coinsurance, and deductibles. You can see any doctor in the country who accepts Medicare with no referrals or prior authorizations needed. Medicare Advantage (Part C) replaces Original Medicare entirely with an all-in-one plan from a private insurer. These plans often include extras like dental, vision, hearing, and fitness benefits, but typically require you to use in-network providers. Medigap gives you maximum freedom and predictable costs. Medicare Advantage gives you more benefits at a lower monthly premium, but with network restrictions. The right choice depends on your healthcare usage, travel habits, and whether you value flexibility or extra benefits.
If you choose Original Medicare with a Medigap supplement, you will need a standalone Part D plan to cover your prescription drugs — Medigap plans do not include drug coverage. If you choose Medicare Advantage, most plans already include Part D drug coverage built in, so you would not need a separate plan. Here is the critical detail: if you do not enroll in Part D when you first become eligible and do not have other creditable drug coverage (like from an employer), you will face a late enrollment penalty. That penalty is 1% of the national base premium for every month you delayed, and it lasts for as long as you have Part D coverage. Even if you take no medications today, enrolling in a low-cost Part D plan protects you from that penalty.
Medicare Supplement premiums in Florida vary based on the plan letter, your age, gender, tobacco use, and the carrier you choose. Plan G, which is the most popular Medigap plan, typically ranges from $100 to $300 per month. Plan N is usually $20 to $50 less per month than Plan G, but comes with small copays for office visits and emergency room use. The coverage is standardized by the federal government, meaning Plan G from Aetna covers the exact same things as Plan G from Mutual of Omaha — only the premium differs. He compares rates across all carriers to find you the lowest premium for identical coverage, and can help you switch carriers later if a better rate becomes available.
